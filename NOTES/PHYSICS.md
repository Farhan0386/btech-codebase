# ENGINEERING PHYSICS — EXAM ANSWERS

## Short Answer Questions

Q1. Differentiate between intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors.

Answer:
**Intrinsic Semiconductor:**
- Pure semiconductor without intentional impurities; example **Si**, **Ge**.
- Number of **electrons = holes** (ni); conductivity low and temperature-dependent.
- Charge carriers generated by thermal excitation across band gap **Eg**.

**Extrinsic Semiconductor:**
- Semiconductor **doped** with donors or acceptors to change conductivity.
- Has **majority** and **minority** carriers (n-type → electrons majority; p-type → holes majority).
- Conductivity higher and controlled by dopant concentration.

**Comparison Table:**
| Intrinsic | Extrinsic |
|-----------|-----------|
| Pure crystal | Doped with impurities |
| Electrons = Holes | Majority ≠ Minority |
| Low conductivity | Higher conductivity |

Q2. Explain the formation of depletion region in a p–n junction diode.

Answer:
- When p and n regions are joined, **diffusion** causes electrons to cross to p-side and holes to n-side.
- Near the junction they **recombine**, leaving behind fixed ionized donors (+) on n-side and acceptors (−) on p-side.
- The carrier-free zone with these fixed charges is the **depletion region**; it has an internal electric field.
- The field produces a **built-in potential Vbi** that opposes further diffusion and establishes equilibrium.
- Width of depletion region depends on doping levels and biasing; it acts as an insulating barrier.

Q3. Compare forward biasing and reverse biasing in a diode.

Answer:
- **Forward bias:** Positive terminal to p-side, negative to n-side → **reduces** barrier, depletion width **decreases**, large forward current flows when V > threshold (~0.7 V for Si).
- **Reverse bias:** Positive to n-side, negative to p-side → **increases** barrier, depletion width **increases**, only small reverse saturation current flows until breakdown.
- **Use:** Forward for conduction/rectification; reverse for blocking, photodiodes, and breakdown-based applications.

Q4. What is Fresnel diffraction.

Answer:
- **Fresnel diffraction (near-field)** occurs when source or screen is at **finite distance** from the aperture; wavefronts are **curved**.
- Requires Fresnel integrals; pattern depends on distance and aperture shape.
- Produces complex near-field fringes (e.g., zone plates) and is used when screen is close to aperture.

Q5. What is Fraunhofer diffraction.

Answer:
- **Fraunhofer diffraction (far-field)** occurs when source and screen are effectively at **infinite distances** or when lenses make wavefronts planar.
- Uses plane-wave approximation; pattern is the **Fourier transform** of the aperture function.
- Yields simple analytic results (sinc pattern for single slit) and is common in lab setups using lenses.

Q6. Explain numerical aperture and refractive indices acceptance angle and acceptance cone of core and cladding.

Answer:
- **Core (n1)** and **cladding (n2)** satisfy **n1 > n2** so light can be guided by TIR.
- **Critical angle:** sin θc = n2 / n1. For θ > θc, total internal reflection occurs.
- **Numerical Aperture (NA):** NA = sqrt(n1^2 − n2^2); it measures light-gathering power.
- **Acceptance angle (θa):** θa = sin^{-1}(NA / n0) (n0 ≈ 1 for air); rays within this cone are guided.
- **Acceptance cone:** 3D cone of half-angle θa from the fiber axis; larger NA → easier coupling but more modal dispersion.

Q7. Compare CRT and LCD, LED and OLED display technologies.

Answer:
- **CRT:** Electron beam excites phosphor; bulky, good color and contrast but heavy and power-hungry.
- **LCD (with LED backlight):** Liquid crystals modulate a backlight; thin, energy-efficient, needs backlight so blacks are not perfect.
- **LED (direct):** Discrete LEDs form pixels or backlight; bright with good contrast.
- **OLED:** Organic diodes emit light per pixel; **excellent contrast and viewing angles**, thin and flexible but organic aging and higher cost.

| Feature | CRT | LCD (LED backlight) | LED (direct) | OLED |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Emission | Electron beam | Modulates backlight | Discrete LEDs | Pixel-level emission |
| Thickness | Bulky | Thin | Thin | Very thin/flexible |
| Contrast | Good | Moderate-high | Good | **Excellent** |
| Power | High | Moderate-low | Low | Low |
| Lifespan | Long | Long | Long | Limited (organic) |

Q8. Explain the working principle of fingerprint sensors with one application.

Answer:
- **Working (common types):**
  - **Optical:** Capture reflected image of ridges and valleys using illumination and camera.
  - **Capacitive:** Measure local capacitance differences—**ridges** are closer and change capacitance vs **valleys**.
  - **Ultrasonic:** Map 3D ridge structure using ultrasonic pulse echoes.
- **Processing:** Image acquisition → enhancement → **feature extraction** (minutiae like ridge endings and bifurcations) → template matching.
- **Application:** **Mobile phone authentication** — unlocking device and authorizing payments securely and quickly.

Q9. State the full form of LASER and explain the importance of population inversion.

Answer:
- **Full form:** LASER = **Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation**.
- **Population inversion:** More atoms occupy an excited lasing level than the lower level; **essential** to get net optical gain because stimulated emission must exceed absorption.
- Achieved by **pumping** and use of **metastable states** to store population; without inversion, laser oscillation cannot start.

Q10. Discuss the role of stimulated emission in laser action.

Answer:
- **Stimulated emission:** An incoming photon of energy **hν** stimulates an excited atom to emit a photon identical in **phase, frequency, polarization, and direction**.
- **Role:** It provides **coherent amplification**; in a resonant cavity stimulated photons build up exponentially when gain exceeds losses.
- **Contrast:** Spontaneous emission is random; stimulated emission is coherent and is the basis of laser light.

Q11. Explain the phenomenon of diffraction in a single slit.

Answer:
- When a plane wave passes through a slit of width **a ≈ λ**, the wave spreads and forms a pattern of bright and dark fringes due to interference of secondary wavelets (Huygens principle).
- **Minima condition:** a sin θ = m λ (m = ±1, ±2, ...).
- **Central maximum:** Widest and brightest; side maxima decrease in intensity.
- Intensity distribution follows **I(θ) = I0 [sin(π a sin θ / λ)/(π a sin θ / λ)]^2** (sinc^2 function).

Q12. Discuss the advantages of optical fiber communication.

Answer:
- **High bandwidth** and data rates; supports modern broadband communication.
- **Low attenuation** and long-distance transmission with reduced repeaters (telecom windows at 1310 nm, 1550 nm).
- **Immunity to EMI**, lightweight, small size and high security (hard to tap undetected).
- **Applications:** Internet backbone, medical endoscopy, sensors, LIDAR, and industrial links.

Q13. Explain the importance of coherence in laser light.

Answer:
- **Temporal coherence:** Long coherence time (narrow linewidth) needed for interferometry and high spectral resolution.
- **Spatial coherence:** Uniform phase across beam enables tight focusing and long-range propagation.
- **Importance:** Coherence allows **stable interference**, holography, precision metrology, and high-intensity focusing.

Q14. Discuss the applications of LASER in modern technology.

Answer:
- **Industrial:** Cutting, welding, micromachining.
- **Medical:** LASIK eye surgery, tissue ablation, dental treatments.
- **Communications:** Optical fiber transmitters and amplifiers.
- **Sensing & Measurement:** LIDAR, interferometry, spectroscopy.
- **Consumer:** Barcode scanners, optical storage, printers.

Q15. Explain the working principle of touch screen technology.

Answer:
- **Resistive:** Two conductive layers separated by gap — touch causes contact and change in resistance to locate point.
- **Capacitive:** Touch changes local capacitance of a conductive layer; controller measures coordinate changes; supports multitouch.
- **Infrared/Optical:** IR emitters/detectors create a grid; touch interrupts beams to determine position.
- **Applications:** Smartphones, kiosks, ATMs — intuitive human–machine interface.


## Long Answer Questions

Q1. Explain Young’s double slit experiment.

Answer:
**Purpose & Statement:**
- Demonstrates **interference of light**, proving light has wave nature by producing stable bright and dark fringes.

**Experimental Setup:**
- Monochromatic coherent source (wavelength **λ**) illuminates two narrow slits S1 and S2 separated by distance **d**; screen placed at distance **D** (D >> d).

**Working Principle:**
- Light from S1 and S2 reaches point P on screen. Path difference Δ ≈ d sin θ ≈ d x / D (for small θ).
- **Constructive interference (bright fringe):** d sin θ = m λ, m = 0, ±1, ±2 ...
- **Destructive interference (dark fringe):** d sin θ = (m + 1/2) λ.
- **Fringe width (β):** β = λ D / d — distance between adjacent bright fringes.

**Important Points:**
- **Coherence** between S1 and S2 is essential (constant phase difference).
- Intensity distribution follows I = I0 cos^2(π d sin θ / λ) under small-angle approximation.

**Applications & Conclusion:**
- Used to measure **wavelength**, small distances, and in interferometry; fundamental experiment illustrating superposition and interference.

Q2. Describe Newton’s ring experiment.

Answer:
**Objective:** Measure wavelength of light or test lens curvature by observing concentric interference rings formed by a lens on a flat plate.

**Setup & Observations:**
- A plano-convex lens of radius **R** is placed on a flat glass plate forming a thin air film of varying thickness. Monochromatic light incident normally produces concentric circular fringes (Newton’s rings) in reflected light.

**Theory & Formulas:**
- Reflections from two surfaces produce phase difference; due to one π phase change at one reflection, the condition for **dark rings** in reflected light is:
- 2 t = (m + 1/2) λ, where t is local air-gap thickness.
- For small t, t ≈ r^2 / (2R) → radius of m-th dark ring: r_m^2 = m λ R.
- Wavelength or R can be found from measured ring radii: λ = (r_{m+1}^2 − r_m^2)/R.

**Diagram (text):**
Lens (convex) placed on flat plate → thin air film → concentric rings observed.

**Applications & Conclusion:**
- Used for optical testing, measuring wavelength, surface flatness, and lens curvature.

Q3. Explain the principle of total internal reflection and discuss the working of optical fiber.

Answer:
**Total Internal Reflection (TIR):**
- When light travels from medium of refractive index **n1** to lower index **n2** (n1 > n2), beyond the **critical angle θc** where sin θc = n2/n1, all light is reflected back — this is TIR.

**Optical Fiber Working:**
- Structure: **Core (n1)** and **cladding (n2 < n1)**; protective coating outside.
- Light entering core within **acceptance angle θa** is refracted into core and repeatedly undergoes **TIR** at core–cladding interface, propagating along the fiber.
- **Numerical Aperture:** NA = sqrt(n1^2 − n2^2); **acceptance angle** θa = sin^{-1}(NA/n0) where n0 is outside refractive index (≈1).
- **Modes:** Single-mode fibers (very small core) reduce modal dispersion; multimode fibers allow multiple modes and higher coupling ease but more dispersion.

**Losses & Windows:**
- Attenuation due to absorption, Rayleigh scattering, and bending; minimized at telecom wavelengths (1310 nm, 1550 nm).

**Applications & Conclusion:**
- Optical fibers are essential for long-distance, high-bandwidth, low-interference communications, medical imaging, and sensing.

Q4. Describe the structure and applications of optical fiber.

Answer:
**Structure:**
- **Core:** Central light-carrying region (index n1). Core diameter: single-mode ≈ 8–10 μm, multimode ≈ 50–62.5 μm.
- **Cladding:** Surrounding layer (index n2 < n1) for TIR.
- **Coating/Buffer:** Polymer for mechanical protection.
- **Jacket & Strength Members:** Outer protection and tensile strength (Kevlar, etc.).
- **Profiles:** Step-index (sharp n change) and graded-index (index decreases gradually to reduce modal dispersion).

**Applications:**
- Telecommunications (backbone, metro, access networks).
- Medical (endoscopes), sensors (temperature, strain via fiber Bragg gratings), industrial laser delivery, LIDAR and defense systems.

**Key Parameters:**
- Attenuation (dB/km), bandwidth, dispersion (modal & chromatic), numerical aperture (NA).

**Conclusion:**
- Optical fiber is compact, high-capacity, and immune to EMI; it underpins modern communication networks and many sensing applications.

Q5. Discuss the conditions necessary for achieving laser action.

Answer:
**Necessary Conditions:**
- **Pumping:** External energy source (optical/electrical/chemical) to excite atoms to higher levels.
- **Population inversion:** More atoms in upper lasing level than lower one — essential for net gain.
- **Stimulated emission:** Incident photons should trigger coherent emission from excited atoms.
- **Optical resonator:** Mirrors (one partially reflective) provide feedback so light makes multiple passes, amplifying via stimulated emission.
- **Gain > Losses (threshold):** Round-trip amplification must exceed cavity losses for oscillation.
- **Metastable state:** A long-lived upper level helps accumulate population for inversion (preferred in 3- or 4-level systems).

**Conclusion:**
- Laser action results from pumping, establishing inversion, and providing optical feedback so that stimulated emission dominates and coherent light is emitted.

Q6. Compare laser light with ordinary light.

Answer:
- **Coherence:** Laser light is highly spatially and temporally coherent; ordinary light is incoherent.
- **Monochromaticity:** Laser is nearly single-wavelength; ordinary sources are broad-spectrum.
- **Directionality:** Lasers produce highly collimated beams; ordinary light is diffuse.
- **Intensity:** Laser beams are very bright and can be focused to small spots; ordinary light is lower intensity.
- **Phase:** Laser photons have fixed phase relation enabling interference applications; ordinary light has random phase.

Q7. Explain the concepts of population inversion and metastable state in LASER systems.

Answer:
**Population Inversion:**
- Condition where the number of atoms in the excited lasing level exceeds that in the lower level; necessary so stimulated emission can dominate over absorption.

**Metastable State:**
- An excited state with relatively long lifetime that allows population to accumulate, making inversion easier to achieve.

**Schemes:**
- **Three-level system:** Pump to higher level, non-radiative decay to metastable level, lasing to ground — high pump required.
- **Four-level system:** Lasing between two excited states; lower lasing level quickly decays to ground making inversion easier and threshold lower.

Q8. Describe the working mechanism of holography with suitable diagram.

Answer:
**Definition:**
- Holography records both **amplitude and phase** of object wavefronts using interference with a reference beam, enabling 3D reconstruction.

**Recording (Steps):**
- Split coherent laser beam into **object beam** (illuminates object; scattered light carries object information) and **reference beam**.
- On the holographic plate, object and reference beams **interfere**, producing an interference pattern (fringes) that encodes amplitude & phase.

**Reconstruction:**
- Illuminating the developed hologram with the original reference beam diffracts light from the interference fringes and **recreates the object wavefront**, producing a virtual 3D image.

**Diagram (text):**
Laser → beam splitter → Reference beam → holographic plate ← Object beam ← object

**Important Notes:**
- **Coherence** is essential (laser source). Recording stores phase via interference; reconstruction uses diffraction from recorded fringes.

Q9. Explain the role of interference and diffraction in holography.

Answer:
- **Interference:** Creates and records the pattern on the hologram by superposing reference and object waves; this pattern encodes both phase and amplitude.
- **Diffraction:** During reconstruction, the recorded interference fringes act as a diffraction grating to regenerate the original object wavefront and produce the 3D image.
- **Combined effect:** Interference stores the necessary phase information; diffraction reconstructs the wavefront from that stored information.

Q10. Analyze the conditions for maxima and minima in single slit diffraction.

Answer:
**Minima (dark fringes):**
- For a slit of width **a**, minima occur at **a sin θ = m λ** (m = ±1, ±2, ...).

**Maxima (bright fringes):**
- Principal maxima lie between minima and are described by the **sinc^2** intensity distribution:
- I(θ) = I0 [sin(π a sin θ / λ)/(π a sin θ / λ)]^2.

**Observations:**
- Central maximum is the brightest and widest; side maxima decrease in intensity roughly as 1/m^2.

Q11. Discuss the working and applications of biometric fingerprint sensors.

Answer:
**Working Summary:**
- **Acquisition:** Optical/capacitive/ultrasonic sensors acquire fingerprint image or 3D map.
- **Preprocessing:** Enhance image, remove noise.
- **Feature extraction:** Identify minutiae (ridge endings, bifurcations) and singular points.
- **Matching:** Compare extracted template with stored templates to authenticate.

**Applications:**
- Device unlocking, secure payments, access control, forensic identification, time & attendance.

Q12. Explain the advantages and limitations of modern display technologies.

Answer:
**Advantages:**
- Thin, high-resolution displays with wide color gamut; energy-efficient options (LED/OLED); fast response times for video and gaming.

**Limitations:**
- LCD lacks true blacks due to backlight; OLED has limited organic lifetime and potential burn-in; cost and ambient reflection are considerations.

**Conclusion:**
- Displays are chosen by trade-offs: contrast and color (OLED) vs cost and brightness (LCD/LED).
